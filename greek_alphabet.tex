Wikipedia (\href{http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Main\_Page}{Online)},
Collins English Dictionary (\href{http://www.collinsdictionary.com/dictionary/english}{Online}),

New Oxford American Dictionary (accessed through Apple's Dictionary software),
The Merriam-Webster Dictionary (Print);
(2) \href{http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English\_pronunciation\_of\_Greek\_letters}{the Wikipedia entry for ``English pronunciation of Greek letters''}; and
(3) \url{http://www.valdosta.edu/~cbarnbau/personal/teaching\_MISC/greek.htm}.}

\footnotetext[3]{The ``th'' is soft as in the English ``thin'', not as in ``they''.}

\footnotetext[4]{As evident in the IPA transcription, ``uh.u'' represents a schwa immediately followed by a near-close near-back rounded vowel (found in the English ``put''), and is equivalent to the UK ``long O''. The dot was infixed so as not to be misinterpreted as [\textipa{UhU}].}
